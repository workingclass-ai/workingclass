# 跳槽 / 留任决策框架 / Stay-or-Leave Framework

This is the reference for the `/jump` command. A multi-dimensional analytical framework for stay-or-leave decisions, replacing company rhetoric, social pressure, and short-term emotion with clear-eyed analysis.

---

## Five Dimensions

A stay-or-leave decision is not "stay vs. leave"，it's a multi-dimensional optimization problem.

1. **Value flow**: Of value you create, how much flows to you?
2. **Irreplaceability trajectory**: Will you be more or less replaceable in 3-5 years here?
3. **Promise fulfillment history**: Company's track record on past commitments?
4. **Opportunity cost**: How much does staying cost you?
5. **Real constraints**: How free are you actually to leave?

---

## Dimension 1: 价值流向 / Value Flow

### How much value do you create?

Quick estimate for a typical engineer:

```
Team total output / Team headcount = Per-person value created
```

Or more precisely:

```
Revenue/cost-saving directly attributed to your work = Your direct value
```

### Example

You're a backend engineer earning $150K. Your team has 10 engineers. Team's product generates $10M revenue/year.

- Per-person value ≈ $1M/year
- Your share ≈ $200K (salary + benefits)
- Company's profit from you ≈ $800K/year

That's: **80% of your created value flows to the company, 20% to you.**

### Is this reasonable?

History and economics: 80/20 is common in capital-labor distribution.

But this is **not natural law**, it's political choice.

In the 19th century, US workers received only 5% of value created. 100 years of labor movements pushed to roughly 30%. Different countries, industries, and companies vary widely.

### Application

When considering a job change, compare value flow at current vs. potential job:

**Current**: X% of value flows to you, salary = Y
**Potential**: X'% of value flows to you, salary = Y'

Decision logic:
- If X' > X AND Y' > Y → clearly jump
- If Y' > Y but you'll create more value too → look at absolute income
- If Y' = Y but X' > X (same pay, better split) → suggests new company respects workers more, long-term should jump
- If Y' > Y but new company's value creation is unclear → use other dimensions

## Dimension 2: 不可替代性轨迹 / Irreplaceability Trajectory

### Core question

In 3-5 years at your current company, will you become **more** or **less** replaceable?

### Positive factors (becoming less replaceable)

- Access to core clients, key products, unique data
- Accumulating domain knowledge others can't quickly replicate
- Growing internal network
- Company investing in your training, new projects, expanded scope
- Working on company's core direction, not edge functions

### Negative factors (becoming more replaceable)

- Your workflows being systematically "documented," "standardized"
- Company using AI to replace your work
- Your scope shrinking
- Working on non-core areas being cut
- Your team shrinking, remaining people doing "maintenance"

### AI-era special dimension

2026's particularity: many things that traditionally built irreplaceability (deep system knowledge, product expertise, domain knowledge) are rapidly becoming replaceable in the AI era，because AI can read all your accumulated documents in 1 day.

So when assessing trajectory, ask a new question:

**What you're accumulating，is it AI-replicable in 5 years, or AI-resistant in 5 years?**

AI can replicate (high risk):
- Mastery of a specific tech stack
- Familiarity with a specific product architecture
- Solutions to specific customer problems

AI can't replicate in 5 years (low risk):
- Trust relationships with specific clients
- Judgment in ambiguous political environments
- Cross-disciplinary, cross-cultural complex judgment
- Chemistry with specific team members
- Truly original insights (not synthesizing existing information)

If your past 3 years accumulated mostly the first kind, seriously consider jumping.
If mostly the second kind, staying may still be valuable.

## Dimension 3: 承诺兑现历史 / Promise Fulfillment History

### Method

List all past company promises to you (or to peers at your level). Calculate fulfillment rate.

### Common promise types

- "Next year's review we'll consider promotion"
- "Special bonus after project completes"
- "Stock will rise after new product launch"
- "I'll fully recommend you"
- "Next quarter we'll reassess your salary"
- "When financial situation improves we'll raise"

### Fulfillment rate calculation

For each promise:
1. Was it fulfilled?
2. If fulfilled, fully or shrunken?
3. If not, what was the company's reason? Reasonable?

### Insights from history

- 70%+ fulfillment → company is reasonably trustworthy, staying may be reasonable
- 30-70% → warning zone
- Below 30% → company manages by hollow promises

### Collective dimension

Don't only look at promises to you. Look at all peers.

If 5 colleagues at your level were told "you'll be promoted this year" but only 1 actually was, real fulfillment rate is 20%.

Don't assume you'll be the exception.

## Dimension 4: 机会成本 / Opportunity Cost

### Cost of staying in a job you shouldn't

Each extra year staying in wrong job costs you:

1. **Salary gap**: What you should earn vs. what you do earn. Usually 20-30%.
2. **Career capital gap**: What you could build elsewhere vs. what you're building here.
3. **Network gap**: People you could meet vs. trapped in current network.
4. **Mental health cost**: Long-term unfair environment affects your psyche.

### Age-band adjustment

- 25-30: easy to jump, many opportunities. Cost of staying in bad job: very high.
- 30-40: moderate jump difficulty. If family stable and savings exist, still high cost.
- 40-50: harder to jump, more selective employers. Need more precise judgment.
- 50+: very hard to jump. Balance risk and opportunity cost carefully.

### Critical question

**5 years from now, looking back at today's decision, will I thank myself for staying, or regret not leaving sooner?**

This question's answer is often more accurate than all specific analyses.

## Dimension 5: 真实约束 / Real Constraints

The most overlooked dimension.

### Financial
- How much savings?
- How many months of runway?
- Debt size (mortgage, car, credit cards)?

### Family
- Are you the primary income?
- Care responsibilities (parents, kids, sick partner)?
- Partner's job flexibility?

### Visa/Immigration (especially relevant for overseas Chinese)
- Does your visa depend on this employer?
- Waiting on green card/PR? Is company sponsoring?
- Job change implications for visa?

### Health
- Health insurance dependency on this job?
- Ongoing medical needs for self/family?

### Mental
- Already burnt out?
- Energy to adapt to a new environment?

### Constraint principles

1. **Don't pretend constraints don't exist**. "Should jump" and "can jump" are different questions.
2. **Don't let constraints be exaggerated**. Some are real (visa). Some are inflated ("I don't have time to interview").
3. **If constraints prevent immediate jumping, plan to reduce them**. Save more, research visa options, stabilize family.
4. **Accept compromise**. Sometimes you must stay in a bad job temporarily. Not failure，reality.

## Decision Matrix

Score 1-5 each (5 = worst):

| Dimension | Score | Weight |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Value flow | ? | Medium |
| Irreplaceability trajectory | ? | High |
| Promise fulfillment | ? | High |
| Opportunity cost | ? | High |
| Real constraints | ? | **Decisive** |

### Recommendation

**If constraint score ≤ 2 (mostly free to choose)**:
- Total 5-10: stay, possibly good company
- Total 11-15: observe, start quietly looking outside
- Total 16-20: actively job search
- Total 21-25: urgently find new job

**If constraint score 3-4 (limited choice)**:
- Total 5-10: stay, but watch external market
- Total 11-15: save money, reduce constraints, prepare for future jump
- Total 16-20: plan exit path, may need 6-12 months
- Total 21-25: emergency, may need to make compromised quick decision

**If constraint score 5 (truly cannot leave now)**:
- Don't pretend you can leave. But do two things:
  1. Minimize current pain (set boundaries, protect health, maintain mental state)
  2. Systematically reduce constraints (save, fix visa, stabilize family)
- Once constraints reduce, immediately re-evaluate

## Special Topic: Counter-offers

### When you have an external offer and company tries to retain

Common retention rhetoric:
- "We've always valued you"
- "I'll fight for a raise for you"
- "You don't need to leave, we'll solve your problems"
- "Trust our commitment to you"

### The truth

HR research data: **70% of employees who accept counter-offers leave within 18 months，but this time involuntarily, not by choice.**

Why?

Once company knows you wanted to leave, they put you on a "flight risk" list. Short-term they retain with raises. Long-term they find replacement. Once replacement is in place, they ask you to leave.

### Application principles

1. **If you've decided to jump, don't let counter-offer change you**，unless counter-offer far exceeds new offer AND company gives written long-term commitments.
2. **If using external offer as leverage to force a raise**, understand you're now on the flight risk list. Consequences appear in 18 months.
3. **If genuinely uncertain whether to leave**, don't use external offers as "test." Decide first, then act.

## Jump Timing Strategy

### Best timing
1. Just after annual bonus paid，maximize value extracted
2. After completing a major project，best resume material
3. Hiring season (spring, fall)，more opportunities
4. Industry investment wave，stronger negotiation position

### Worst timing
1. During mass-layoff wave in industry，few opportunities, weak negotiation
2. 1-2 months before annual bonus，you'll lose it
3. Before promised promotion/stock vesting，you'll lose it

### Underrated timing: Before being laid off (proactively)

If your company starts large-scale layoffs (not just individuals), even if you're not on current list, proactive jumping may beat waiting to be laid off:

- Proactive: your story is "I wanted better opportunity"
- Reactive (laid off): your story is "I got laid off"
- The latter is a burden in future interviews

But this trades against severance. Calculate carefully.

## A Director's Honest Observations

I've managed 40+ engineers. Watched many job decisions.

Patterns I observe:

1. **Early jumpers are usually right**. From first thought "should I leave" to actually leaving averages 18 months. In those 18 months, market value declines.

2. **Procrastinators usually regret**. "Wait one more year" is the largest source of career losses.

3. **Counter-offers should almost never be accepted**. Except in very specific situations.

4. **Well-prepared people negotiate much better**. Last-minute job hunters get bargaining power suppressed.

5. **People with external offers negotiate 10x stronger**. Even if they ultimately stay, hand having external offer beats hand without.

The most important point:

**Always maintain your "employability."** Look at external market at least once a year (even if not planning to jump). Talk to external companies at least once a year. This isn't disloyalty，it's basic self-protection.

People who spent careers at one company are most painful when finally laid off. No external network, no updated resume, no market awareness.

Don't be that person.

---

## Summary

Jumping isn't running away. Staying isn't loyalty.

Both are investment decisions about your specific moment, for your future.

Analyze clearly, evaluate honestly, act decisively.

Remember: when your employer makes decisions about you, they're never emotionally captured. They look at data, ROI, market.

You should too.
