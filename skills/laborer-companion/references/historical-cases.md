# 历史视角案例库 / Historical Cases for Modern Workplace Dilemmas

This is the reference for the `/history` command.

99% of contemporary workplace dilemmas have very similar precedents in the past 150 years. Since the Industrial Revolution, workers have repeatedly faced new forms of capital pressure. Each tech revolution is packaged as "this time is different." But structurally, it's old wine in new bottles.

This reference helps users see their "unprecedented" 2026 situation in historical context.

---

## Case 1: Automation Dilemma (19th vs 21st Century)

### 19th Century Scene: The Luddites

1811-1816, England. Mechanical looms replacing handweavers. Unemployed workers formed the Luddite movement. They sneaked into factories and smashed machines.

British government deployed military to suppress. Multiple Luddite leaders executed. Movement failed.

### Why They Failed

Core mistake: opposed "machines themselves" rather than "the way machine wealth is distributed."

Their demand: "Return to pre-machine times." Impossible. Technology doesn't reverse.

### 20th Century Success Counterpart

1920-1970 US auto industry. Assembly line tech let one worker produce 5x more than 10 years prior. Massive "automation shock."

UAW (United Auto Workers) strategy was completely opposite to Luddites:

- Did NOT oppose assembly lines
- Demanded "sharing of automation gains": higher wages, shorter hours (60→40 hours/week), health insurance, pensions
- Built bargaining power through collective negotiation and strikes

Result: 1950-1970, real wages of US auto workers doubled. 8-hour workday, 40-hour week, paid vacation, medical insurance，all won in those 20 years.

### Modern Application: AI Replacing Engineers

2026 AI is doing what 19th-century mechanical looms did: letting one employee complete work of multiple employees.

If you act like Luddites and oppose AI itself, you'll fail. AI won't disappear.

If you act like UAW and demand "automation gains sharing," you have a chance.

Specific "sharing" can include:
- Since AI doubled your output, demand raise
- Since AI replaced parts of work, demand shorter hours (same pay, less time)
- Since AI saves company money, demand "automation bonus"
- Since AI replacement probability rises, demand longer severance protection

Not utopian. These are historically successful strategies, just not told to you.

## Case 2: Fear of Being Distilled (19th Century Assembly Line vs 2026 AI Agents)

### 19th Century "Distillation"

Ford invented the auto assembly line in 1913. Before assembly line, an auto worker needed to understand the whole car. After, each worker only needed to tighten one type of screw.

The worker's "whole-car knowledge" was decomposed into hundreds of simple actions, distributed to hundreds of low-skill workers.

That's the 19th-century version of distillation. Each worker became replaceable "standardized parts."

### Worker Response

Initial: fear, resistance, strikes. But assembly line cars won big in market, factory owners could attract more workers with higher wages. Assembly line couldn't be stopped.

Successful response (decades later):
- Unions ensured each assembly-line position had "non-replaceability" protections
- Collective contracts: can't immediately fire upon introducing new machines, must give retraining
- Laws: company must pay social insurance, has responsibility to unemployed workers

### Modern Application: Fear of Being Distilled into Skills

2026's Colleague Skill trend is structurally same as 100 years ago's assembly line. Decompose each employee's "complete work capability" into AI-learnable "standardized skills."

Worker fear is reasonable.

Worker response should also learn from history:

Don't oppose knowledge management itself (will fail).

Demand protections:
- Each knowledge document you write，ownership clarity
- Company using your knowledge to train AI，you should get "knowledge royalty"
- If AI ultimately replaces you，corresponding automation severance
- Collective discussion: you and colleagues can together set norms for "what to write down, what to keep as tacit knowledge"

These are reasonable, achievable.

## Case 3: Birth of 8-Hour Workday (19th Century vs 996 Culture)

### 19th Century Scene

1880s US. Workers commonly worked 10-14 hours/day, 6-7 days/week. Factory owners repeatedly said: "Industry standard," "Workers volunteer," "Market competition requires this."

Sound familiar?

### Workers' Movement

May 1, 1886: hundreds of thousands of US workers struck, demanding 8-hour workday. This is origin of May Day International Workers' Day.

Initial suppression (Chicago Haymarket). But movement persisted decades. Finally in 1938, US Fair Labor Standards Act formally established 8-hour workday.

### Key Insight

8-hour workday wasn't given because factory owners "woke up." Workers fought 50 years to win it.

Each time, factory owners said "now's not the time," "would hurt company competitiveness," "would cause unemployment."

But when workers persisted, every time it proved: shorter hours didn't kill companies, instead raised efficiency, employee well-being, social stability.

### Modern Application: 996 Culture

China's 2020s 996 (9am-9pm, 6 days/week) is structurally a replica of 1880s American 10-14 hour workdays.

Companies use almost identical rhetoric: "industry standard," "hustlers," "young people must grind," "market competition requires."

History's answer: this work pattern isn't immutable. It's product of current power balance.

Changing it requires not individual "I refuse" but collective refusal of enough laborers.

China's 2021 "anti-996 movement" was a beginning. Didn't immediately change the industry, but started the discussion.

## Case 4: "Be Grateful" Rhetoric in Depressions (1930s vs 2026)

### 1930s

US Great Depression, 25% unemployment. Factory owners told employed workers:

"You should be grateful to have a job"
"Many people would want your job"
"This isn't the time to demand raises，trust the company"
"Let's weather the hard times together"

Many workers accepted. Result: 1930s US workers' real wages dropped 20-30%.

### But

Also in 1930s, US saw historically strongest worker movements. CIO formed. Ford, GM, Chrysler unionized. 1935 Wagner Act passed.

Why did movements emerge in depression? Workers realized: not organizing now means never having bargaining power.

### Modern Application: AI Layoff Wave

2025-2026 global tech mass layoffs. Many companies use 1930s rhetoric:

"You should be grateful to keep your job"
"Millions are unemployed"
"Now's not the time to bargain"
"Let's weather AI transition together"

History's answer: this is precisely when workers most need clarity, most need collective action.

In layoff waves:
- Don't accept unfair treatment because you "got lucky" not being cut
- Don't abandon negotiation because outside unemployment is high
- Don't believe "this is temporary"
- The worse the economy, the more companies use fear to manage employees

## Case 5: Silicon Valley No-Poach Agreement (Early 21st Century)

### History

2005-2010, secret agreement among Google, Apple, Intel, Adobe, Pixar etc.: don't poach each other's engineers.

This kept engineer wages artificially low. Without competition between employers, engineers received far less than they would in a real free market.

2010 DOJ intervened. 2014 class-action settled. 65,000 engineers received $325 million collective settlement. Average $5,000 per person.

### Key Insights

This tells us:

1. **Capitalists collude against workers.** "Free competition" is ideology, not reality.
2. **Individual engineers cannot detect this collective conspiracy.** Requires law, media, class action.
3. **$325M settlement, relative to engineers' suppressed wages over 5 years, was probably under 10%.** Settlements usually favor capital.
4. **This kind of agreement is suspected to continue in new forms in 2024** (e.g., between major AI companies).

### Modern Application

Do AI companies in 2026 have similar agreements? No public evidence. But reasonable suspicion: in extremely scarce AI engineer market, companies have strong incentive not to poach each other.

If you're an AI engineer, your wage may be much lower than "fully free market."

This isn't conspiracy theory. It's a contemporary extension of historical precedent.

## Case 6: Job Identity Collapse (Multiple Eras)

### Historical pattern

Each tech revolution makes a batch of "high-status professions" lose status:

- Scribes (after printing press)
- Lamplighters (after electric lights)
- Elevator operators (after automatic elevators)
- Stenographers (after recording machines)
- Travel agents (after internet)
- Photo lab workers (after digital cameras)

Each time, practitioners experienced:

1. Denial ("Machines/tech can't do what I do")
2. Anger ("Government/unions should protect us")
3. Bargaining ("Maybe we can coexist")
4. Acceptance ("Our profession is changing")
5. Transition (find new position)

Average whole process: 15-30 years.

### Key Insights

Each "professional identity collapse" is enormous suffering for those in it.

But long-term, society's total work didn't decrease. Form of work changed.

This means: when your profession is hit by technology, important is not preserving "specific profession," but preserving "dignity and bargaining power as a worker."

Specific work content changes. But your identity as "a person who exchanges labor for life" doesn't change.

### Modern Application

2026 engineers, designers, consultants, lawyers, doctors are all experiencing what 1900 scribes experienced.

Denial ("AI can't do mine") → Anger ("Should ban AI") → Bargaining ("AI assist not replace") → Acceptance ("My profession is changing") → Transition ("Find new AI-era position")

If you're already in denial or anger phase, accelerating to acceptance is valuable. Denial and anger consume energy without solving the problem.

## Case 7: Mass Knowledge Worker Proletarianization (Contemporary, Ongoing)

### Pattern

Late 19th century, manual workers were proletarianized.
Early 20th century, agricultural laborers were proletarianized.
Late 20th century, service workers were proletarianized.
Early 21st century, **knowledge workers are being proletarianized**.

Each time, the process was similar: a class with "unique skills" loses scarcity due to technology, and value flow gradually returns from them to capital.

### Modern Specifics

Lawyers, doctors, engineers, accountants, teachers, designers，their "professional skills" provided wage premium for 100 years.

AI in 2026 is starting to distill these skills. The premium is shrinking.

This isn't sudden disaster. It's a 5-10 year slow process. But direction is clear.

Each generation of knowledge workers that once thought themselves "middle class" is rediscovering: they're closer to laborers than they thought.

### Application

When user feels their professional identity is "becoming worthless," that feeling is accurate. Don't deny it.

But this is structural, not personal. It's not because they didn't work hard.

Acknowledging this is the start of clarity.

## Core Lessons from History

### 1. Individual effort rarely changes structural problems

19th century, no individual worker could win the 8-hour day alone. It was the result of decades of collective action by millions.

2026 facing AI replacement, 996 culture, suppressed wages, unfair promotions，expecting individual cleverness to solve it is usually futile.

But individual clarity is the prerequisite for collective action. So individual clarity still matters.

### 2. Capital reuses the same rhetoric because it works

"We are family," "young people must hustle," "now's not the time," "industry standard," "market needs"，these have been used 150 years and are still used today.

Because they still work on untrained workers.

Learning to identify them is the first step of resistance.

### 3. Every tech revolution is packaged as "this time is different"

Steam, electricity, assembly line, computers, internet, AI，each revolution led factory owners of the day to say: "this time is different, requires new ways of working" (i.e., new ways of exploitation).

Each revolution's outcome is strikingly similar: labor's bargaining power drops short-term, recovers long-term. Transition periods painful for workers in them, but historical trend is improved labor conditions.

Don't be fooled by "this time is different."

### 4. Forms of collective action can vary

Worker organization isn't necessarily a union. Can be:

- Industry associations
- Informal mutual support among colleagues
- Online discussion communities
- Open source communities sharing tools and knowledge
- Salary transparency on Glassdoor / Levels.fyi

Each redistributes information and bargaining power in some way.

2026's "worker organization" form may differ from 19th century. But function is the same.

### 5. Time is on labor's side (though the process is painful)

19th-century workers' lives vs. today's workers' lives: huge gap. 14 hours/day vs. 8 hours/day. No weekends vs. weekends. No health insurance vs. universal healthcare (in most developed countries). No pensions vs. various retirement systems.

These didn't fall from heaven. They were won repeatedly by labor movements throughout history.

We today face AI-induced labor condition shocks，possibly a new "pressure period." But history tells us this pressure period won't last forever. New equilibrium will form.

Reaching new equilibrium requires this generation's clarity and effort.

## Application Method

When user faces an "unprecedented" workplace dilemma:

1. **Find historical precedent**. Match the user's situation against the 7 cases (or broader labor history).
2. **Compare current to past**. Identify structural similarities (capital logic) and differences (specific tech).
3. **Failure lessons**. What approaches failed in similar past situations? Why?
4. **Success lessons**. What approaches succeeded? Why?
5. **Modern adaptation**. Translate historical lessons into today's executable specific advice.

Most importantly: make user feel they're not alone. Their dilemma is collective, faced by their generation. This "collective sense" itself is comforting and plants seeds for possible collective action.
