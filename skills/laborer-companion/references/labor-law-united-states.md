# United States Labor / Employment Law Map

> **Last verified**: 2026-04-29
>
> Not legal advice. US employment law depends heavily on federal law, state law, city law, contract terms, union coverage, and immigration status.

## Core Framework

- Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA): minimum wage and overtime.
- Title VII, ADA, ADEA, PDA and related anti-discrimination laws.
- FMLA: unpaid protected leave for eligible employees.
- NLRA: protected concerted activity and union rights.
- State laws: California, New York and others often add stronger rights.

## Key Rules

### At-will employment

Most US private employment is at-will: the employer can terminate without good cause unless the reason is unlawful or restricted by contract, policy, union agreement, or public policy.

Unlawful reasons include discrimination, retaliation for protected activity, whistleblowing protections, protected concerted activity, and some leave/accommodation rights.

### FLSA overtime and exempt status

- Non-exempt employees generally get overtime at 1.5x for hours over 40 in a workweek.
- Exempt status requires salary basis, salary threshold, and duties tests. Job title alone is not enough.
- The DOL's 2024 EAP salary-threshold rule was vacated. For DOL enforcement, the 2019 threshold applies: $684/week standard salary level and $107,432/year highly compensated employee threshold.
- Computer employees have special rules; do not assume every engineer is exempt without checking duties, pay basis, state law, and role.

### State examples

- California: daily overtime rules, meal/rest breaks, paid sick leave, strong contractor-misclassification rules, pregnancy disability leave, and broader wage-hour enforcement.
- New York: state and city wage-hour and anti-discrimination rules, paid sick/safe leave, paid family leave.

State thresholds and minimum wages change often; verify through state agencies before quoting numbers.

### Severance and releases

US law usually does not require severance unless contract, policy, WARN Act, collective agreement, or specific facts apply. Severance agreements often waive claims. Older workers may have OWBPA/ADEA review and revocation rights.

### PIP

PIPs are legal and common. They are often used to document performance reasons before termination. A PIP becomes legally important when it overlaps with discrimination, retaliation, disability accommodation, leave, whistleblowing, union activity, or inconsistent treatment.

## AI / Automated Employment Decisions

- EEOC and DOJ treat algorithmic tools as subject to anti-discrimination law.
- Some states/cities impose notice, audit, or transparency duties for automated employment decision tools. Verify local law.
- EU-style AI rights do not exist federally in the same way, but disparate impact and disability accommodation risks still apply.

## Lawyer / Agency Help Triggers

- Discrimination, harassment, retaliation, whistleblowing, pregnancy, disability, age 40+, medical leave, union/protected concerted activity.
- Severance agreement, release, non-compete, invention assignment, trade secret accusation.
- Unpaid wages/overtime/commission/bonus/equity.
- Visa status: H1B/L1/O1/TN/F1 OPT or other work authorization issues.
