# European Union Labour Law Map

> **Last verified**: 2026-04-29
>
> Not legal advice. EU law sets minimum rules, but employment rights are implemented through member-state law and collective agreements.

## Core Framework

- EU directives create minimum standards; member states implement them differently.
- Key areas: working time, health and safety, consultation, equality/non-discrimination, fixed-term/part-time/temporary agency work, whistleblowing, data protection, and AI.

## EU-Level Baselines

### Working time

The Working Time Directive requires member states to provide:

- Average weekly working time limit of 48 hours including overtime.
- Rest break if working more than 6 hours.
- Minimum 11 consecutive hours daily rest.
- Minimum weekly rest.
- At least 4 weeks paid annual leave.
- Night-work protections.

Member states can have stronger rules, collective agreements, and sector-specific rules.

### Dismissal and consultation

Dismissal law is mostly national. Collective redundancy, works councils, information/consultation, and transfer of undertakings rules can be strong, but details vary.

### AI / automated workplace decisions

- EU AI Act classifies some AI systems used in employment and worker management as high-risk.
- Workplace emotion recognition has significant restrictions/prohibitions.
- GDPR may provide rights around automated decision-making, workplace monitoring, data access, and lawful basis for processing.
- Application dates and guidance are staggered; verify current Commission and national guidance.

## Country Pointers

### Germany

- Strong dismissal protection when Kündigungsschutzgesetz applies: usually 6+ months service and employer size threshold.
- Works councils (Betriebsrat) can materially affect process.
- Dismissal must be socially justified where protection applies.

### France

- Strong dismissal procedure and justification requirements.
- 35-hour reference workweek; overtime rules and collective agreements matter.
- Right to disconnect exists in French labour framework.

### Netherlands

- Employer often needs UWV or court route for dismissal.
- Transition payment and strict process rules can matter.
